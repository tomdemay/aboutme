About Me

Allow me to introduce myself. My name is Tom DeMay, and I am a Senior Software Engineer currently employed at Amazon AWS. I would like to share a bit about my background and explain why I believe I would be a valuable addition to any organization.

Professional Experience

With nearly 30 years of experience as a Software Engineer, I have accumulated a diverse range of expertise. Throughout my career, I have served in various roles, including individual contributor, technical lead, and management positions. While I possess a deep understanding of coding, I also thrive as a problem solver. No matter the challenge at hand, I take full ownership, from inception to completion, leveraging my ability to build the necessary tools, systems, and teams required to achieve the desired goals.

I am particularly passionate about the software development process and continuously seek ways to enhance efficiency, write maintainable code, improve team and organizational communication, deliver consistent and reliable estimates, and mentor junior developers. As I acquire domain knowledge, I naturally transition into a technical leadership role. My previous employers and colleagues hold me in high regard, appreciating my commitment to refactoring and my collaborative approach when working alongside other engineers and managers.

My insatiable appetite for learning has always driven me to acquire new skills. For instance, shortly after completing high school in 1989, in just under 3 months I obtained a series 6 and 63 license to sell securities. In 2000, I earned a private pilot's license in a span of three months. In addition to my accomplishments outside of software engineering, I am entirely self-taught in the field of software engineering. By my late 20s, I had already assumed engineering management roles, published works on software engineering principles, and delivered speeches at local user group meetings. At the age of 30, I pursued a computer science degree, completing four years of coursework in just one year while maintaining a GPA of 4.0. In 2003, I embarked on building www.discountcasinogear.com as an experimental project to learn how to build web sites, which eventually became one of the leading sites in its industry, consistently generating annual revenue in the range of half a million dollars until I successfully sold it for a profit in 2011. The site, still operational, proudly asserts its status as the largest supplier of gaming equipment, outlasting many of its competitors.

My reputation is built on maintaining high standards, crafting well-designed and maintainable code, and effectively collaborating with others. I have rescued several failing projects, receiving numerous accolades for my efforts, and hold a patent. Mentoring and developing the skills of junior and struggling engineers are among my passions. Refactoring code is a task I relish, having achieved remarkable performance improvements, such as a 3,951% boost in one instance. Previously, code that originally took 6 hours to run before crashing due to resource consumption was refactored to complete in just 6 minutes, utilizing only 40MB of memory.

As evident from my journey, I possess an unwavering commitment to continuous education. I have taught myself a multitude of technologies, including C/C++, .NET, C#, Windows and Linux platforms, Java, Python, PowerShell, bash, Ruby, Regular Expressions, classic ASP, HTML/CSS, SQL and No-SQL databases, and cloud computing, to name a few. Currently, I am actively honing my skills in front-end web development using Java Spring, Spring Boot, Angular, React.js, MVC, and LAMP stacks. I am also working towards attaining my AWS and Azure certifications, with future plans to delve into machine learning and AI.

Finding My Path

I went straight to work after high school as I was just too immature to continue my education in college having been a very poor student in high school. I graduated with a 1.7 GPA. My parents had me tested and determined I was just bored. I could do the work. I just didn't want to. Going to college then would have been a waste of money. Especially considering that I didn't know what I wanted to do for a career.

Personal computers were just starting to become affordable for wide spread use. My Dad bought an 8088 and used it quite often. In 10th grade I took an intro to programming course where we were taught basic programming. The teacher was new to the subject being a math teacher with no computer experience. So he was learning with us. But he couldn't learn at the pace This was one class I was passionate about and did rather well in. The next year I took a Cobal programming course.

Back then you were considered a Nerd if you were into computers. So I resisted and pushed back against my Dad who often told me I will be a software engineer. So I bounced around for a while. When I was 19 I thought I might want to be a stock broker. So I picked up some books, and took the tests and within 2-3 months I had obtained my series 6 and 63 license. But no one wanted financial advice from an immature 19 year old punk kid so that didn't work out too well.

I went on to work in two different banks, a stock broker firm as a cashier and then an administrative assistant at Construction Specifications Institute where I got the opportunity to pose as a hand model for an add running in their magazine. It was kind of fun. It was there I started to play around with programming. I started writing applications for myself in basic and interacting with their mainframes.

After my boss and I were laid off she put me in touch with her son who managed a team doing computer sales to the federal government. I joined the team but was soon frustrated by the lack of software tools for the sales force to use. Our vendor's would send us their pricing on a disk every month and it was just thrown in the trash. We were selling Fox Software Fox Pro, so I was able to get a free copy and I started to write database applications. I wrote something to manage my contacts, pricing, a quotation system and an order entry system. I found my path. I finally accepted I was a developer down at heart and accepted a role at the company creating tools for the whole company to use. In my early 20's I found myself managing 4 people building internal systems. I was on my way.

My Software Engineering Journey

Microsoft bought Fox Software to acquire their Rushmore technology to use to build Microsoft SQL Server and eventually released Microsoft Visual FoxPro. It was my first introduction to object oriented programming. I was hooked. I marketed myself hard in the small FoxPro community. I wrote articles for Advisor Publications, even getting the cover once. I spoke at user group meetings. But FoxPro was considered a 2nd rate programming language. So I wanted to learn more. I started teaching myself C/C++ and left the job to pursue an opportunity to work alongside some highly recognized names in the FoxPro community. It was there I was hoping to learn about System Design and Architecture. Client server databases were relatively new then. I started writing applications with Microsoft SQL Server on the back end. I wrote a few articles in Advisor Publications and spoke at a local user group about how to do it.

I had no formal software engineering training. I was slinging code and I became very anxious to learn. I started writing other articles and pursued my new dream of becoming a Software Engineer hard. I eventaully got my first break as a C/C++ engineer in my early 30's working for a company that was eventually acquired by EMC (which is now part of Dell). I developed a reputation of being a hard worker, a skilled engineer and by my early 30s I found myself managing again, leading a team of 6 people developing a library that 200 other EMC developers would use to build the applications on top of. This was a very high profile job and very rewarding. It was here I got my first patent.

One of the benefits EMC's offered employees was continued education reimbursement. I took advantage of that and at 30, I went back to school to get my Bachelor's in Computer Information Systems. I was eager to get my degree quickly so I taught myself most of the material and took CLEP tests, tested out of classes, or put portfolios together demonstrated I knew the material being taugh so I could get life experience credits to get credit for the class. I completed 4 years of school and graduated Suma Cum Laude with a 4.0 average a year later.

I worked at EMC for nearly 12 years. Moved on to a small start up company before finding myself working at Amazon AWS. Along the way I taught myself various other technologies and programming languages. My passion is refactoring code, mentoring more junior developers and just owning the whole software delivery process.

The rest is just history.... I was on my way.

Discount Casino Gear

I had started building gaming tables for me and my friends and decided I would build a web site to sell my tables. It wasn't long before an importer found me and asked to partner up. Within 6 months I had launched Discount Casino Gear generating half a million in revenue consistently year after year until I sold it for a profit in 2011.

I had launched Discount Casino Gear a year before the poker craze really took off. I was spending everything I made on cost per click advertising. I had no real overhead as my site was fully automated, sending orders to my supplier who dropped shipped product for me, sending out shipping information to customers and even automated building new pages when new products where released and even scrubbing my competitors web sites every night and programmatically adjusted pricing to undercut them. All I had to do was customer service. The site was entrenched and competitors came and went and Discount Casino Gear is now one of the leading gaming supply web sites.
